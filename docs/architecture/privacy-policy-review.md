## Issues and Gaps

### 1. Analytics Not Disclosed

Section 2.3 mentions collecting "Usage Data: Pages visited, time spent on pages, and interaction patterns" but doesn't disclose **how** this data is collected.

**Questions to address**:
- Is Google Analytics, Plausible, PostHog, Vercel Analytics, or similar in use?
- If yes, add them to Section 4.1 with privacy policy links
- If no third-party analytics (just server logs), clarify that

### 2. Cookie Consent Mechanism Missing

For GDPR compliance, a cookie consent banner/mechanism is needed that:
- Appears before non-essential cookies are set
- Allows users to accept/reject categories of cookies
- Remembers their choice

The policy describes cookies but doesn't mention how users consent before they're set.

**Action**: Implement a cookie consent banner for EU visitors (or all visitors for simplicity).

### 3. GPC Signal - Is It Actually Implemented?

Section 6.3 claims the site honors Global Privacy Control signals. This needs to be actually implemented in the frontend code.

**Action**: Verify the frontend checks for `navigator.globalPrivacyControl` and acts accordingly (e.g., disabling any non-essential tracking).

### 4. Data Export Feature

Section 6.1 promises data portability ("Request an export of your data in a machine-readable format").

**Action**: Make sure this feature is built, or have a manual process documented to handle requests.

### 5. No Data Breach Notification Policy

The policy doesn't mention what happens if there's a security breach.

- GDPR requires notifying authorities within 72 hours and affected users "without undue delay"
- CCPA requires notification in "the most expedient time possible"

**Consider adding** a section like:
> "In the event of a data breach that affects your personal information, we will notify you via email and/or through a notice on our website within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach, as required by applicable law."

### 6. International Transfer Mechanism Weak

Section 10 says users "consent" to international transfers by using the service. Under GDPR, consent alone is often insufficient for international data transfers. The standard mechanisms are:

- Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)
- Adequacy decisions
- Binding Corporate Rules

For a small project, this is low risk, but technically GDPR requires more than just "consent to transfer."

**Consider**: Adding language like "We rely on Standard Contractual Clauses approved by the European Commission for transfers to our service providers."

### 7. No Physical/Mailing Address

CCPA and GDPR prefer (CCPA) or may require (GDPR for certain situations) a physical address for privacy requests. Currently only email is provided.

**Consider**: Adding a mailing address, even if it's a PO Box or registered agent address.

### 8. GDPR Data Controller Identification

For GDPR, the policy should explicitly identify the business as the "data controller" and, if outside the EEA and processing EEA data, an EU representative may be needed.

For a small side project, this is low risk, but the formal requirement exists.

### 9. Error Tracking / Monitoring Services

If Sentry, LogRocket, or similar error tracking is used, these services receive user data (IP, browser info, sometimes user actions) and should be disclosed.

## Minor Items

### 10. Sensitive Personal Information Definition

Section 6.2 mentions "sensitive personal information" includes "email and account credentials." Under CCPA, sensitive PI has a specific legal definition (SSN, financial accounts, precise geolocation, racial/ethnic origin, etc.). Email and passwords aren't technically "sensitive PI" under CCPA.

This isn't harmful, but it's imprecise.

### 11. Retention of Anonymized Data

Section 5 says anonymized data may be retained indefinitely. This is fine, but ensure anonymization is true anonymization (not just removing names but keeping unique identifiers that could re-identify users).
